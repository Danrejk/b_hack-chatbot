# Emergency Contact Numbers by Country/Region

Generic local emergency numbers for civil-protection guidance. Always confirm
the user's country before citing a number, and default to the EU-wide 112 if
the country is unknown or unclear.

## European Union (general)

- **All emergencies (police, fire, ambulance):** 112 — works in every EU member
  state, including Germany, Denmark, Estonia, Finland, Poland, Lithuania, and
  Sweden.

## Germany

- **Police:** 110
- **Fire / Ambulance / Medical emergency:** 112
- **Poison control (Giftnotruf, national):** +49 30 19240
- **Non-emergency police contact:** local Polizeidirektion via 110 switchboard

## Denmark

- **All emergencies:** 112
- **Non-emergency police:** 114

## Estonia

- **All emergencies:** 112

## Finland

- **All emergencies:** 112

## Poland

- **All emergencies:** 112
- **Police (national):** 997
- **Fire:** 998
- **Ambulance:** 999

## Lithuania

- **All emergencies:** 112

## Sweden

- **All emergencies:** 112
- **Non-emergency police:** 114 14

## Russia (Kaliningrad and other regions)

- **All emergencies (mobile, unified):** 112
- **Police:** 102
- **Fire:** 101
- **Ambulance:** 103

## General guidance for the assistant

- Always lead with the single unified emergency number for the user's
  country if you are unsure which service they need.
- If the user's country cannot be determined, give the EU-wide 112 and ask
  which country/region they are in so you can refine the number.
- Never recommend a specific third-party app to place the call or find a
  contact — describe the action generically (e.g. "call 112 from your
  phone's dialer", "use your phone's built-in contacts").
